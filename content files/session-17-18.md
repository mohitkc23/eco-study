## 1. From Closed to Open Economy Macroeconomics

### 1.1 The Fundamental Identity Revisited

In a **closed economy:** Y = C + I + G

In an **open economy:** Y = C + I + G + NX (where NX = X − M)

The addition of net exports (NX) is what makes open economy macroeconomics different:
- Fiscal and monetary policies now have **spillover effects** on the exchange rate
- Exchange rate changes feed back into **output and employment**
- **Capital flows** (financial account) link domestic interest rates to the global market

### 1.2 The Three Policy Objectives in an Open Economy

Every government wants, simultaneously:
1. **Domestic monetary policy autonomy** — set interest rates to manage inflation/output
2. **Exchange rate stability** — predictable exchange rate for trade and investment
3. **Free capital mobility** — allow capital to flow in and out without restriction

The **Impossible Trinity** (Trilemma) says you can have at most **two of these three** at any time.

## 2. The Impossible Trinity (Mundell's Trilemma)

### 2.1 The Core Framework

```
                    MONETARY POLICY AUTONOMY
                    (Set your own interest rates)
                           /\
                          /  \
                         /    \
                        /      \
                       / YOU CAN\
                      / HAVE ONLY \
                     /   TWO OF   \
                    /    THREE     \
                   /________________\
  EXCHANGE RATE                   CAPITAL MOBILITY
  STABILITY                       (Free flow of capital)
  (Fixed rate)                    across borders
```

### 2.2 The Three Possible Policy Combinations

| Combination | Countries that Chose This | What You Give Up |
|---|---|---|
| **Monetary Autonomy + Fixed Rate** → sacrifice Capital Mobility | China (historically), Malaysia 1998, India (partially) | Free capital flows — impose capital controls |
| **Monetary Autonomy + Capital Mobility** → sacrifice Fixed Rate | USA, UK, India (currently), most emerging markets | Exchange rate stability — float the currency |
| **Fixed Rate + Capital Mobility** → sacrifice Monetary Autonomy | Eurozone countries, Hong Kong Currency Board, Panama | Independent monetary policy — must follow anchor currency's rates |

### 2.3 Country Case Studies

#### The Eurozone — Fixed Rate + Capital Mobility = No Monetary Autonomy
- Euro nations have a **common currency** (ultimate fixed rate vis-à-vis each other)
- **Free capital flows** within the Eurozone
- But they **surrendered monetary policy** to the ECB (European Central Bank)
- Problem during 2010-12 Eurozone crisis: Greece could not devalue to restore competitiveness; could not lower interest rates independently

#### China — Fixed Rate + Monetary Autonomy = Capital Controls
- China historically pegged the yuan to the USD within a band
- Maintained independent monetary policy (set rates for domestic conditions)
- But imposed **strict capital controls** — prevented free outflow of capital
- This is why the Renminbi has not become a reserve currency despite China's trade dominance

#### India — Monetary Autonomy + Capital Mobility = Managed Float
- RBI sets interest rates independently (monetary autonomy)
- Capital flows (FDI and some FII) are relatively free
- But exchange rate is not fixed — rupee floats (with occasional intervention)

#### USA — Monetary Autonomy + Capital Mobility = Floating Rate
- Fed sets rates for domestic purposes
- No capital controls
- Dollar floats against all major currencies (since 1973)
- This is the "unconstrained" corner — possible only because the USD IS the reserve currency

## 3. The Mundell-Fleming Model

### 3.1 What Is the Mundell-Fleming Model?

The **Mundell-Fleming Model** extends the **IS-LM framework** to an open economy with capital mobility. It analyses the effectiveness of **fiscal policy** and **monetary policy** under different exchange rate regimes.

### 3.2 The Three Curves

```
IS* curve (Goods Market + NX):
  Output Y is determined by: C + I + G + NX
  NX depends on the exchange rate e
  Higher e (depreciation) → cheaper exports → NX rises → IS shifts right
  Higher interest rate r → lower I → IS shifts left

LM* curve (Money Market):
  Money demand = Money supply
  At higher r, money demand falls → equilibrium at higher Y
  Domestic interest rate = world interest rate r* (with perfect capital mobility)

BP curve (Balance of Payments):
  BP = CA + KA = 0 at equilibrium
  At world interest rate r*, BP balances at exchange rate e*
```

### 3.3 Policy Effectiveness Summary

| Policy | **Floating Rate Regime** | **Fixed Rate Regime** |
|---|---|---|
| **Fiscal Policy (↑G)** | **Ineffective** (Complete crowding out via exchange rate appreciation) | **Effective** (Multiplier works fully) |
| **Monetary Policy (↑Ms)** | **Effective** (Exchange rate depreciates → NX rises → output rises) | **Ineffective** (Must sell money/buy currency to defend rate → money supply reverses) |

#### Why Fiscal Policy is Ineffective Under Floating Rates:
1. Government increases spending ↑G → IS shifts right → Y wants to rise
2. Higher Y → higher money demand → r rises above r*
3. Capital inflows (seeking higher r) → exchange rate appreciates
4. Appreciation → NX falls → IS shifts back left → offset completely
5. Net result: **exchange rate appreciates, NX falls, Y unchanged**

#### Why Monetary Policy is Effective Under Floating Rates:
1. Central bank expands money supply → LM shifts right → r falls below r*
2. Capital outflows (seeking higher r elsewhere) → exchange rate depreciates
3. Depreciation → NX rises → IS shifts right → Y rises
4. Net result: **output increases, exchange rate depreciates, NX improves**

#### Why Monetary Policy is Ineffective Under Fixed Rates:
1. Central bank tries to expand money supply → r falls below r*
2. Capital outflows → exchange rate wants to depreciate
3. Central bank must intervene: **buy domestic currency / sell foreign currency**
4. This reduces money supply back to original level → LM shifts back
5. Net result: **money supply unchanged, output unchanged**

## 4. Fixed vs Floating Exchange Rate Regimes — The Big Debate

### 4.1 Arguments for a Fixed Exchange Rate

| Argument | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Reduces uncertainty** | Predictable exchange rate lowers transaction costs for trade and investment |
| **Discipline** | Forces domestic inflation to match the anchor currency → imposes fiscal/monetary discipline |
| **Credibility** | Currency board arrangements (e.g., Hong Kong) eliminate speculative attacks |
| **Trade promotion** | Reduces exchange rate risk → encourages long-term trade contracts |
| **Small open economies** | For very small economies heavily dependent on one trading partner, fixing makes sense |

### 4.2 Arguments for a Floating Exchange Rate

| Argument | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Automatic adjustment** | Trade imbalances self-correct — deficit → depreciation → exports rise |
| **Monetary policy freedom** | Can set interest rates for domestic conditions |
| **Absorbs external shocks** | Oil price spike → currency depreciates → competitiveness maintained |
| **No speculative attacks** | No "fixed rate to defend" → cannot be attacked |
| **Unlimited reserves not needed** | Don't need large forex reserves to defend a rate |

### 4.3 The Optimal Currency Area (OCA) Theory

> **Optimal Currency Area (OCA):** A region is an optimal currency area (best suited for a common currency or fixed exchange rate) if it has:
> 1. **High factor mobility** (workers and capital can move freely)
> 2. **Synchronised business cycles** (countries experience booms and recessions together)
> 3. **Fiscal transfer mechanism** (richer regions can support poorer ones)
> 4. **Similar economic structures** (similar response to external shocks)

**The Eurozone crisis (2010-12) as a failure of OCA:**
- Greece and Germany did NOT meet OCA criteria — very different economic structures
- Without the ability to devalue, Greece faced prolonged depression
- Without fiscal transfers (Germany reluctant), adjustment was extremely painful

## 5. Output and Employment Under Different Regimes

### 5.1 The Aggregate Demand Channel

Exchange rate → Net Exports → Aggregate Demand → Output → Employment

```
Depreciation:
e ↑ (₹/$ rises) 
→ Exports cheaper in foreign currency → export demand ↑ (X ↑)
→ Imports more expensive domestically → import demand ↓ (M ↓)  
→ NX = X − M improves
→ AD = C + I + G + NX rises
→ Output Y rises
→ Employment rises
```

### 5.2 The Inflation Trade-off

Depreciation also:
- Makes **imported goods more expensive** in domestic currency → imported inflation ↑
- Raises cost of imported inputs → **cost-push inflation** in manufacturing
- Weakens the real wage of workers

**The dilemma:** Depreciation helps exporters and employment but worsens inflation and real purchasing power — a classic **output-inflation trade-off**.

### 5.3 The Expenditure-Switching vs Expenditure-Reducing Approaches

Two ways to correct a current account deficit:

| Approach | Mechanism | Trade-off |
|---|---|---|
| **Expenditure Switching** | Depreciate currency → make imports expensive, exports cheap → switch demand from imports to domestics | Works if Marshall-Lerner holds; takes time (J-curve); causes inflation |
| **Expenditure Reducing** | Cut domestic spending (fiscal austerity, tight money) → reduce demand for imports → CAD falls | Reduces output and employment (recession risk); deflationary |

**IMF structural adjustment programs** typically use both, creating the painful "twin adjustment" — austerity + currency devaluation.

## 6. Open Economy Macroeconomics — The Full Identity System

### 6.1 The Core Macroeconomic Identities (Review from Sessions 1-2)

```
Y = C + I + G + NX                          [1] GDP Identity

S_pvt = I + NX + (G−T)                      [2] Uses of Private Saving

(M−X) = (G−T) + (I − S_pvt)                 [3] CA Deficit = Domestic Spending > Income

CA + KA + FA = 0                            [4] BoP Balance (accounting identity)
```

### 6.2 The Twin Deficits Hypothesis

From Identity [3]:
```
Current Account Deficit = Government Budget Deficit + Private Sector Deficit
```

**Twin Deficits:** When a government runs large fiscal deficits (G > T), this tends to create or worsen the current account deficit (M > X) — because fiscal expansion boosts income and demand, some of which spills into imports.

**US example:** The Reagan-era fiscal expansion (1980s) coincided with a large current account deficit → "twin deficits" entered the economic lexicon.

**India example:** Pre-1991, large fiscal deficits financed by money printing → inflation → real appreciation → exports uncompetitive → current account deficit → BoP crisis.

### 6.3 The Trilemma and Domestic Policy Space

The Impossible Trinity directly constrains what a government can do:

```
IF you want to:                    THEN you must:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Fight inflation with high rates    Accept currency appreciation
  (and have open capital)         and weaker exports

Maintain export competitiveness   Accept higher inflation
  (via weak currency)             or impose capital controls

Have exchange rate stability       Lose monetary policy control
  with open capital account        (follow anchor country's rates)
```

## 8. Exchange Rate Regimes: Modern Examples and Lessons

### 8.1 The Asian Financial Crisis (1997-98) — Lessons

- **Thailand, South Korea, Indonesia, Malaysia** had **de facto fixed exchange rates** to the USD with open capital accounts
- They attracted large capital inflows (hot money seeking higher rates)
- When confidence broke → **speculative attacks** on currencies → rapid depreciation → financial crisis
- **Loss:** Couldn't defend the peg; couldn't use monetary policy; capital controls came too late

**Lesson:** Fixed rates + open capital account = vulnerability to sudden stops

### 8.2 China's Gradual Opening

- China maintained strict capital controls (sacrificing capital mobility) to retain both **monetary autonomy** (set PBOC rates for domestic conditions) and **exchange rate stability** (pegged CNY to USD)
- Gradual liberalisation since 2015, with the CNY now in a managed float band
- Tension: China wants Renminbi internationalisation (capital mobility) but fears loss of control

### 8.3 India's Post-1991 Approach

India moved from a **fixed rate** to a **managed float** after 1993:
- Maintained monetary autonomy (RBI sets repo rate for inflation target)
- Partial capital account openness (FDI free; portfolio flows regulated; full capital account convertibility NOT yet)
- Exchange rate floats but RBI intervenes to reduce volatility
- India has deliberately maintained **partial capital controls** — a middle-path policy consistent with managing the trilemma

## 9. Key Terms and Exam Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Impossible Trinity / Trilemma** | Cannot simultaneously have (1) monetary autonomy, (2) fixed exchange rate, and (3) free capital mobility — can have at most two |
| **Mundell-Fleming Model** | Open-economy IS-LM model showing how fiscal/monetary policy effectiveness depends on the exchange rate regime |
| **IS* curve** | Goods market equilibrium in open economy — NX depends on exchange rate |
| **LM* curve** | Money market equilibrium — with perfect capital mobility, domestic rate = world rate |
| **Twin Deficits** | Fiscal deficit tends to produce/worsen current account deficit |
| **Expenditure Switching** | Using depreciation to redirect demand from imports to domestics |
| **Expenditure Reducing** | Austerity to compress imports |
| **Optimal Currency Area** | Region best suited for common currency: high labour mobility, synchronised cycles, fiscal transfers |
| **Currency Board** | Ultimate fixed rate — 100% reserve backing of currency in circulation (Hong Kong example) |
| **Capital Controls** | Restrictions on inflow/outflow of financial capital across borders |
