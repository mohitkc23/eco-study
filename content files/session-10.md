## 1. Pre-Trade Markets — Setting the Stage

### 1.1 The Bike Market Example

Two countries trade in **bicycles** (the product is just a teaching device — the logic applies to any good).

#### USA Market (Pre-Trade)
- **Demand:** P = −Q + 400
- **Supply:** P = Q + 100
- **Equilibrium:** Q* = 150, P* = 250
- Equilibrium: **EQ*_USA = (150, 250)**

#### Indian Market (Pre-Trade)
- **Demand:** P = −0.5Q + 250
- **Supply:** P = 0.5Q + 50
- **Equilibrium:** Q* = 200, P* = 150
- Equilibrium: **EQ*_IND = (200, 150)**

**Key observation:** Pre-trade price in **USA (250)** > Pre-trade price in **India (150)**. This price differential creates the incentive for trade — India will export to the USA.

```
USA: High-price market → will IMPORT from India (demand pent-up at lower prices)
India: Low-price market → will EXPORT to USA (supply pent-up at higher prices)
```

### 1.2 Pre-Trade Surpluses

> **Consumer Surplus (CS)** = Difference between the highest price consumers are willing to pay and the actual price they pay. Graphically: area between the demand curve and the market price (above P*, below demand curve).

> **Producer Surplus (PS)** = Difference between the market price and the lowest price producers are willing to accept. Graphically: area between market price and supply curve (above supply curve, below P*).

**Calculating Pre-Trade CS and PS:**

For the USA:
```
CS_USA = ½ × Base × Height = ½ × 150 × (400 − 250) = ½ × 150 × 150 = 11,250
PS_USA = ½ × Base × Height = ½ × 150 × (250 − 100) = ½ × 150 × 150 = 11,250
```

For India:
```
CS_IND = ½ × 200 × (250 − 150) = ½ × 200 × 100 = 10,000
PS_IND = ½ × 200 × (150 − 50) = ½ × 200 × 100 = 10,000
```

| Country | Consumer Surplus (Pre-Trade) | Producer Surplus (Pre-Trade) |
|---|---|---|
| **USA** | 11,250 | 11,250 |
| **India** | 10,000 | 10,000 |

## 2. Post-Opening of Trade — Deriving the International Market

### 2.1 Import Demand Curve (USA)

In the USA, there is **pent-up demand** below the equilibrium price of 250 — consumers who couldn't buy at that price but would buy from the international market at a lower price.

```
Import Demand = Q_D_USA − Q_S_USA
= (−P + 400) − (P − 100)
= −2P + 500
⟹ P = −0.5Q + 250                ... (1)
```

**Interpretation:** The Import Demand curve starts at P = 250 (no imports at US autarky price) and slopes downward.

### 2.2 Export Supply Curve (India)

In India, there is **pent-up supply** above the equilibrium price of 150 — producers who couldn't sell profitably at 150 but would sell at a higher international price.

```
Export Supply = Q_S_IND − Q_D_IND
= (2P − 100) − (−2P + 500)
= 4P − 600
⟹ P = 0.25Q + 150               ... (2)
```

**Interpretation:** The Export Supply curve starts at P = 150 (no exports at Indian autarky price) and slopes upward.

### 2.3 The International Equilibrium Price

Setting Import Demand = Export Supply:
```
−0.5Q + 250 = 0.25Q + 150
100 = 0.75Q
Q* = 400/3 ≈ 133.3 units

P* = 0.25 × (400/3) + 150 = 100/3 + 150 = 550/3 ≈ 183.3
```

**International Equilibrium:** P*_INTL = **550/3 ≈ $183.3**

> **Key result:** The international price lands **between** the two countries' autarky prices:
> **150 < 550/3 < 250** ✓

## 3. Redistribution of Surpluses After Trade

### 3.1 The Step-by-Step Logic

1. The international price is **P*_INTL = 550/3 ≈ 183.3**
2. **USA story:** US autarky price (250) > international price (183.3) → US consumers want to import at the cheaper price. Domestic US producers must now sell at the international price.
3. **India story:** Indian autarky price (150) < international price (183.3) → Indian producers want to export. This raises prices for Indian domestic consumers.

### 3.2 The Post-Trade Diagram Analysis

```
USA (Post-Trade)                   International Market
P                                  P
│                                  │
250─── EQ_USA                      250─ D_Imports
│      ↑                           │
550/3───────── P*_INTL             550/3────── EQ*
│     c│a│e│ b│d                   │       b+d
│      │ │ │  │                    │         n
100─── │ │ │  │                    150─ S_Exports
│      │ │ │  │                    │
───────────────                    ─────────────
  250/3   150  650/3  Q              0      400/3  Q
           Imports→
```

### 3.3 Gains and Losses Table

| | USA (Importing) | | | India (Exporting) | | |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| | **Pre-Trade** | **Post-Trade** | **Net Effect** | **Pre-Trade** | **Post-Trade** | **Net Effect** |
| **Consumers** | c | c + a + (b+d) | **+(a+b+d)** | i+j+k | i | **−(j+k)** |
| **Producers** | a+e | e | **−a** | l+m | l+m+(j+k)+n | **+(j+k+n)** |
| **Net (C+P)** | c+a+e | a+b+c+d+e | **+(b+d)** | i+j+k+l+m | i+j+k+l+m+n | **+n** |

**Summary of effects:**
- **USA Consumers:** GAIN (area a+b+d) — lower prices + more quantity
- **USA Producers:** LOSE (area a) — forced to sell at lower international price
- **USA Net:** GAIN (b+d) — efficiency gains from trade
- **India Consumers:** LOSE (j+k) — forced to pay higher international price
- **India Producers:** GAIN (j+k+n) — can sell at higher international price + access new buyers
- **India Net:** GAIN (n) — exporter's surplus from international buyers

### 3.4 Which Country Gains More?

> **Compare (b+d) [USA gain] vs (n) [India gain]:**
> - If **(b+d) > n** → USA benefits more from trade
> - This occurs if the **Import Demand curve is more elastic** than the Export Supply curve in the international market
>
> Yet, **in totality, both countries benefit positively** — the sum of national consumer and producer surpluses rises in both countries.
>
> → This is the core argument: **trade benefits every country** at the national level, though there may be winners and losers **within** each country.

## 4. Tariffs and Their Impact on Trade

### 4.1 What is a Tariff?

> A **tariff** is a tax imposed by a government on **imported goods**, aimed at protecting domestic industries or generating revenue.

**Types of tariffs:**

| Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Ad valorem** | Charged as a **percentage** of the value of imported goods | 10% tax on the price |
| **Specific** | Charged as a **fixed amount per unit** regardless of price | $5 per imported item |
| **Compound** | Combines ad valorem + specific | 5% tax PLUS $2 per unit |

### 4.2 Deadweight Loss from a Tariff

Using the USA market from our example, suppose a tariff raises the international price in the USA to P_post-tariff = 222 (up from 183.3):

```
USA Market — Post-Tariff:
P
│
250─── EQ_USA
│     x│a│e│ b│d
222─────────────────── Post-tariff price
│     │ │ │  │
550/3──────────────── Pre-tariff int'l price
│           f │ c
100─── supply
│
────────────────────── Q
  (250/3)  150  (650/3)
```

### 4.3 Welfare Effects of a Tariff — Full Table

| Actor | Pre-Tariff | Post-Tariff | **Net Gain/Loss** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Consumer Surplus** | x + f + a + b + c + d | x + f | **−(a+b+c+d)** |
| **Producer Surplus** | e | a + e | **+a** |
| **Government Revenue** | 0 | c | **+c** |
| **Net National W/L** | x+f+a+b+c+d+e | x+f+a+e+c | **−(b+d)** |

**Key insight:** Areas **(b+d) are Deadweight Loss** — they disappear from consumer surplus but are NOT captured by producers or government. They are pure waste.

Where:
- **b** = production distortion loss (resources wasted producing domestically what could be imported cheaply)
- **d** = consumption distortion loss (consumers priced out of the market who would have bought at free trade prices)
- **c** = government tariff revenue (redistribution from consumers to government)
- **a** = producer subsidy effect (redistribution from consumers to domestic producers)

> **Bottom line:** On a net basis, tariffs make the **importing country worse off** — the gains to producers and government are outweighed by consumer losses, leaving a net deadweight loss of (b+d).

## 5. The Gains from Trade — Offer Curves and Terms of Trade

### 5.1 The Evaluating Benefits Framework (PPT Summary Table)

| | **USA (Importing)** | | **RoW (Exporting)** | |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| | **No Trade** | **Free Trade** | **Net Effect** | **Net Effect** |
| Consumers | c | a+b+c+d | **a+b+d** | **−(j+k)** |
| Producers | a+e | e | **−a** | **j+k+n** |
| Net Effect | c+a+e | a+b+c+d+e | **b+d** | **n** |

### 5.2 Offer Curves

> **Offer curves** (reciprocal demand curves), introduced by **Marshall and Edgeworth**, show how much of its **import commodity** a nation demands in exchange for supplying various amounts of its **export commodity**.

The intersection of two nations' offer curves determines the **equilibrium terms of trade**.

### 5.3 Terms of Trade

```
Terms of Trade (ToT) = P_X / P_Y = Export Price Index / Import Price Index
```

**India's Terms of Trade:** Historically impacted by the composition of exports (commodity-heavy) vs imports (oil, capital goods). A deterioration in ToT means India gets less imports for each unit of exports.

## 6. Production Possibilities with Increasing Costs

Unlike Ricardo's constant-cost model, the real world has **increasing opportunity costs**:

- The **PPF is bowed outward (concave to origin)**
- **Marginal Rate of Transformation (MRT)** = slope of PPF = opportunity cost
- MRT **increases** as more units of Good X are produced (reflecting diminishing returns as resources are pulled from Y to X)

**Key point:** Under increasing costs, nations will **not fully specialise** (unlike the constant-cost case). They will produce some of both goods, but trade allows consumption beyond the PPF.

---

## 7. The Edgeworth Box

The **Edgeworth Box** is a graphical tool showing the distribution of two goods between two parties (or two nations). The **contract curve** (locus of Pareto-optimal allocations) shows all efficient distribution points inside the box where no one can be made better off without making the other worse off.

In the trade context, the Edgeworth Box shows the **pre-trade production allocation** within a nation across two industries.

## 9. HBS Case Context: Angola and the Resource Curse

**Case:** Angola and the Resource Curse (Musacchio, Werker, Schlefer, HBS N9-711-016)

**Core concepts illustrated:**
- **Dutch Disease:** When a country discovers a valuable natural resource (oil, minerals), the export earnings can cause the real exchange rate to appreciate, making non-resource exports uncompetitive and hollowing out manufacturing
- **Resource Curse:** Countries with abundant natural resources often experience worse economic growth and governance outcomes than resource-poor countries (paradox of plenty)
- **Terms of Trade:** Countries dependent on commodity exports face volatile ToT due to commodity price cycles
- **Balanced vs Biased growth:** Natural resource booms can lead to biased growth (only the resource sector grows) rather than balanced growth across the economy

**Angola specifics:**
- Heavily dependent on oil exports
- Oil revenue funded civil war and then reconstruction
- Manufacturing sector atrophied due to Dutch Disease dynamics
- Governance and institutional quality challenges

---

## 10. Balanced vs Biased Growth and Terms of Trade

**Balanced Growth:** When a country's productive capacity grows uniformly across both export and import-competing sectors.

**Biased Growth:** When growth is concentrated in one sector — particularly the export sector.

**Impact on Terms of Trade:**
- Biased growth in the **export sector** → more exports supplied to world market → **ToT deteriorates** (export prices fall)
- This is known as **"immiserising growth"** — a country can grow and yet become worse off if its ToT deteriorates enough to offset the volume gains

## 11. Exam-Ready Summary

### Key Results

| Scenario | Importing Country | Exporting Country |
|---|---|---|
| **Free Trade** | Net gain = (b+d) | Net gain = (n) |
| **Tariff** | Net loss = (b+d) [deadweight] | Exporter loses exports |
| **No Trade (Autarky)** | Baseline | Baseline |

### Critical Formulas

```
Consumer Surplus = ½ × Q* × (P_max − P*)

Producer Surplus = ½ × Q* × (P* − P_min)

Import Demand = Q_D − Q_S (derives the import demand curve)

Export Supply = Q_S − Q_D (derives the export supply curve)

ToT = P_X / P_Y
```
