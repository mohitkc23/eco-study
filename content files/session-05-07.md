## 1. The Basis for Trade — Overview

Why do nations trade? Several complementary theories explain this:

```
BASIS FOR TRADE
```

## 2. Absolute Advantage (Adam Smith)

### 2.1 The Classic Example

| | U.S. | U.K. |
|---|---|---|
| **Wheat** (bushels/hour) | 6 | 1 |
| **Cloth** (yards/hour) | 4 | 5 |

**Analysis:**
- **U.S. has absolute advantage** over U.K. in **wheat** (produces 6 vs 1 per hour)
- **U.K. has absolute advantage** over U.S. in **cloth** (produces 5 vs 4 per hour)
- **Both nations can gain** from specialising in their absolute advantage and trading

### 2.2 Calculating Gains — Absolute Advantage

**If the US exchanges 6W for 6C:**
- **USA's gain:** Normally, to produce 6C internally, USA spends 1.5 hours (6/4). By trading 6W (produced in 1 hour), USA saves 0.5 hours → **USA gains 6C for the cost of 1 hour instead of 1.5 hours**
- **UK's gain:** Normally, to produce 6W internally, UK spends 6 hours (6/1). By trading 6C (produced in 1.2 hours), UK saves 4.8 hours → **huge efficiency gain for UK**

## 3. Comparative Advantage (David Ricardo)

### 3.1 The More Powerful — and Counterintuitive — Idea

| | U.S. | U.K. |
|---|---|---|
| **Wheat** (bushels/hour) | 6 | 1 |
| **Cloth** (yards/hour) | 4 | 2 |

Notice: **U.K. now has an absolute disadvantage in BOTH goods** — it is less productive in both wheat and cloth compared to the US.

**Yet trade is still beneficial.** Why?

**Analysis:**
- UK labour is **half as productive in cloth** (2 vs 4) but **six times less productive in wheat** (1 vs 6) compared to the US
- UK's relative disadvantage is smaller in cloth → **UK has a comparative advantage in cloth**
- US has a **comparative advantage in wheat**

### 3.2 The Comparative Advantage Law (Ricardo's Statement)

> **"Even if one nation is less efficient than (has absolute disadvantage with respect to) the other nation in production of both commodities, there is still a basis for mutually beneficial trade."**  
> — David Ricardo

### 3.3 Two Ways to Explain Comparative Advantage

#### Labour Theory of Value
> The value or price of a commodity depends **exclusively on the amount of labour** used to produce it.

#### Opportunity Cost Theory (preferred)
> The **opportunity cost** of a good is the amount of a second good that must be given up to release just enough resources to produce one additional unit of the first good.

**Computing Opportunity Costs (from the table above):**
- In the **US:** Opportunity cost of 1 Wheat = 4/6 = 2/3 Cloth. Opportunity cost of 1 Cloth = 6/4 = 1.5 Wheat
- In the **UK:** Opportunity cost of 1 Wheat = 2/1 = 2 Cloth. Opportunity cost of 1 Cloth = 1/2 = 0.5 Wheat

| | Opportunity Cost of Wheat (in Cloth) | Opportunity Cost of Cloth (in Wheat) |
|---|---|---|
| **US** | 2/3 Cloth | 1.5 Wheat |
| **UK** | 2 Cloth | 0.5 Wheat |

- US has **lower opportunity cost for wheat** → US specialises in Wheat
- UK has **lower opportunity cost for cloth** → UK specialises in Cloth

### 3.4 Range for Mutually Beneficial Trade

With the original comparative advantage table:

1. Both countries can gain from trade
2. The **US gains** as long as 6W trades for **more than 4C** (its internal rate: 6W = 4C)
3. The **UK gains** as long as 2C trades for **more than 2W** (its internal rate: 1W = 2C, so 2C = 1W)
4. Therefore, the **range for mutually beneficial trade is:**

```
4C < 6W < 12C
```

(Any trade ratio within this range benefits both countries)

### 3.5 Comparative Advantage With Money

**Assumptions:**
- US wage rate = **$6/hour**; UK wage rate = **£1/hour**
- Exchange rate = **$1 = £2**

| | US | UK |
|---|---|---|
| **Unit Price of Wheat** | $1 (= $6 wage / 6 bushels) | £0.50 = $1/worker-hr × 1 bushel... = $2 at exchange rate |
| **Unit Price of Cloth** | $1.50 (= $6 / 4 yards) | £0.50 = $1 at exchange rate |

**Monetary Price Table (in USD at $1 = £2):**

| | U.S. | U.K. |
|---|---|---|
| **Unit Price Wheat** | $1 | $2 |
| **Unit Price Cloth** | $1.50 | $1 |

**Results:**
- UK cloth is cheaper in dollars → **US imports cloth from UK** ✓
- US wheat is cheaper → **UK imports wheat from US** ✓

**For mutually beneficial trade:** $1 < P_Wheat < $2

> **Key insight:** Changes in **exchange rates** will change the gains from trade and possibly the **patterns of trade**.

## 4. The Production Possibilities Curve (PPC)

### 4.1 Definition

> **Production Possibilities Curve/Frontier (PPC/PPF)** shows alternative combinations of two commodities a nation can produce by **fully using all resources** with the best available technology.

> Without trade, the PPC also represents the **consumption frontier**.

### 4.2 Constant Opportunity Costs (Ricardo's Simple Case)

Constant opportunity costs arise when:
1. Resources are either perfect substitutes for each other OR used in fixed proportion in production of both commodities
2. All units of the same factor are homogeneous

Result: PPC is a **straight line**

### 4.3 Conclusion Under Constant Costs

> Under constant cost conditions, nations will **completely specialise** in their comparative advantage.
>
> With complete specialisation in both nations, the equilibrium-relative commodity price of each commodity lies **between the pre-trade relative commodity price** in each nation.

## 5. Pre-Trade and Post-Trade Analysis (With Increasing Costs)

### 5.1 Pre-Trade (Autarky) Equilibrium

In autarky, each nation produces and consumes at the tangency of its **PPC** and its **community indifference curve (IC)**.

| | Production | | Consumption | | Relative Price (Px/Py) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| | **Good X** | **Good Y** | **Good X** | **Good Y** | |
| **Nation 1** | 50X | 60Y | 50X | 60Y | 1/4 |
| **Nation 2** | 80X | 40Y | 80X | 40Y | 4 |

**Observation:** Nation 1 has a relatively lower price for Good Y (Px/Py = 1/4 means Y is cheap relative to X). Nation 2 has a relatively lower price for Good X (Px/Py = 4 means X is cheap). → **Nation 1 has comparative advantage in Y; Nation 2 in X.**

### 5.2 Post-Trade Equilibrium

With trade, both nations specialise and reach a higher indifference curve:

| | Production | | Consumption | | Relative Price |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| | **Good X** | **Good Y** | **Good X** | **Good Y** | |
| **Nation 1** | 130X | 20Y | 70X | 80Y | 1 |
| **Nation 2** | 40X | 120Y | 100X | 60Y | 1 |

**Balanced Trade check:**
- Nation 1 exports: 130 − 70 = **60 units of X**
- Nation 2 imports: 100 − 40 = **60 units of X** ✓ *(balanced)*

> **Balanced trade condition:** Quantity of X Nation 1 wants to export = Quantity of X Nation 2 wants to import. The **equilibrium relative commodity price** is the common price at which trade is balanced.

### 5.3 Community Indifference Curve

> A **community indifference curve** shows combinations of two commodities that yield **equal satisfaction** to the community/nation.
> - Higher the curve → greater utility
> - **Marginal rate of substitution (MRS)** of X for Y = amount of Y that a nation gives up for one extra unit of X while remaining on the same IC
> - MRS **falls** as more of X is consumed (diminishing marginal utility)

### 5.4 Marginal Rate of Transformation (MRT)

> **MRT** (= slope of PPF) is another name for **opportunity cost**. MRT **increases** as more units of Good X are produced — reflecting increasing opportunity costs.

## 6. Gains from Trade — Welfare Analysis

### 6.1 The Surplus Analysis Framework

| | USA (Importing) | RoW (Exporting) |
|---|---|---|
| | No Trade | Free Trade | Net Effect | No Trade | Free Trade | Net Effect |
| **Consumers** | c | a+b+c+d | **+(a+b+d)** | | | **−(j+k)** |
| **Producers** | a+e | e | **−a** | | | **+(j+k+n)** |
| **Net (C+P)** | c+a+e | a+b+c+d+e | **+(b+d)** | | | **+n** |

**Key takeaways:**
- **Importing country (USA):** Net gain = areas **(b + d)** — these are the efficiency gains from trade
- **Exporting country (RoW):** Net gain = area **(n)** — the gain from selling in the higher-priced international market
- Both countries gain — confirming the fundamental case for free trade

## 7. Offer Curves and Terms of Trade

### 7.1 Offer Curves (Marshall-Edgeworth)

> **Offer curves** (sometimes called *reciprocal demand curves*) show how much of its **import** commodity a nation demands in order to be willing to **supply** various amounts of its **export** commodity.

- Introduced to international economics by **Marshall and Edgeworth**
- Can be derived from: production possibilities frontier + indifference map + various hypothetical relative commodity prices

**Intersection of two nations' offer curves** → determines the **equilibrium terms of trade**

### 7.2 Terms of Trade

> **Terms of Trade (ToT)** is the ratio of the price of a nation's **export** commodity to the price of its **import** commodity. Also called **commodity terms of trade** or **net barter terms of trade**.

```
ToT = P_X / P_Y
```

Where:
- P_X = export price index
- P_Y = import price index

**Interpretation:** If ToT rises → nation gets more imports for each unit of exports → **improvement in welfare**

## 8. Heckscher-Ohlin (H-O) Model

### 8.1 The Core Framework

```
HECKSCHER-OHLIN MODEL
         │
         ├── HO Theorem (Trade pattern prediction)
         └── Factor Price Equalization (Income distribution prediction)
```

### 8.2 The H-O Theorem

> **A nation will export the commodity whose production requires the intensive use of the nation's relatively abundant and cheap factor, and import the commodity whose production requires the intensive use of the nation's relatively scarce and expensive factor.**

**Simple version:** Countries export goods that intensively use their abundant factors.
- **Labour-abundant country** (e.g., India, China) → exports **labour-intensive goods** (textiles, garments)
- **Capital-abundant country** (e.g., USA, Germany) → exports **capital-intensive goods** (aircraft, machinery)

### 8.3 Factor Intensity

> In a two-commodity, two-factor world, commodity Y is **capital-intensive** if the capital-labour ratio **(K/L)** used in production of Y is **greater** than K/L used in production of X.

**Important:** It is not the absolute amount of capital and labour, but the **amount of capital per unit of labour** that determines capital intensity.

### 8.4 Factor Abundance — Two Definitions

#### By Physical Units
> Nation 2 is **capital-abundant** if the ratio of total capital to total labour **(TK/TL)** available in Nation 2 is **greater** than that in Nation 1.

#### By Factor Prices
> Nation 2 is **capital-abundant** if the ratio of the rental price of capital to labour **(P_K/P_L = r/w)** is **lower** in Nation 2 than in Nation 1.

Where:
- **r** = rental rate of capital
- **w** = wage rate

**Important:** It is not the absolute level of r, but the ratio **r/w** that determines whether a nation is capital-abundant.

### 8.5 Factor Price Equalization (Stolper-Samuelson Related)

> **International trade causes wages (w) to rise in Nation 1** (the low-wage, labour-abundant nation) and **fall in Nation 2** (the high-wage nation), reducing pre-trade wage differences.
>
> Similarly, trade causes the **return on capital (r) to fall in Nation 1** (the capital-expensive nation) and **rise in Nation 2** (the capital-cheap nation).

**Mechanism:**
- Trade increases demand for the abundant factor in each country (which was underemployed before trade)
- This drives up its price toward international parity
- Over time, **factor prices converge** across countries — even without migration of workers or capital

**Policy implication (Stolper-Samuelson Theorem):**
- In a labour-abundant country, free trade **benefits workers** but **hurts capital owners**
- In a capital-abundant country, free trade **benefits capital owners** but **hurts workers**
- This is why we observe political opposition to free trade from labour in rich countries

## 9. Revealed Comparative Advantage (RCA) — Balassa Index (1965)

### 9.1 The Concept

> **RCA** measures a country's **share of world exports in one commodity** divided by its **share of total world exports**. It "reveals" comparative advantage from actual trade data, rather than theoretical factor endowments.

```
         [Export of commodity j by country i / Total world exports of j]
RCA_ij = ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
              [Total exports of country i / Total world exports]
```

**Formula in symbols:**
```
RCA = (X_ij / Σ_i X_ij) / (Σ_j X_ij / Σ_i Σ_j X_ij)
```

Where:
- X = exports
- i = index for the **country**
- j = index for the **good/commodity**

**Interpretation:**
- **RCA > 1** → country has a revealed comparative advantage in that commodity
- **RCA < 1** → country does NOT have comparative advantage
- **Range:** 0 < RCA < ∞

### 9.2 Worked Example (from Sample Questions)

| Commodity | India Exports | ROW Exports | RCA (India) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Wine | 5 | 20 | 0.526 |
| Cheese | 8 | 4 | **1.753** ✓ |
| Tomatoes | 12 | 13 | **1.26** ✓ |
| Apples | 13 | 35 | 0.712 |

**India has RCA in Cheese and Tomatoes** (RCA > 1). India does NOT have comparative advantage in Wine or Apples relative to the rest of the world.

## 10. Comparative vs Competitive Advantage

| | Comparative Advantage | Competitive Advantage |
|---|---|---|
| **Origin** | David Ricardo (1817) | Michael Porter (1990) |
| **Based on** | Relative opportunity costs / factor endowments | Firm-level innovation, strategy, industry cluster |
| **Level of analysis** | Country-level | Firm/industry-level |
| **Key word** | *Comparative* | *Competitive* |
| **Mechanism** | Lower opportunity cost of production | Innovation, upgrading, fierce rivalry |

> **Note: The key word is *competitive*, not *comparative*.** — Prof. Kolukuluri (PPT5, re: Porter)

## 12. Practice Problems

**Problem 1 (from Sample Quiz):**

| | Lapland |
|---|---|
| Wool (units/labour hour) | 1.5 |
| Meat (units/labour hour) | 2 |

If Lapland is isolated, what is the exchange rate for 1 unit of Meat (in terms of Wool)?

*Answer:* Opportunity cost of 1 Meat = 2/1.5 = **1.33 Wool** (i.e., to produce 1 more unit of Meat, Lapland must give up 1.33 units of Wool)

**Problem 2 (from Sample Quiz):**

| Country | Labour | Land | Labour/Land Ratio |
|---|---|---|---|
| Galicia | 40 | 6 | 6.67 |
| Crimea | 90 | 8 | 11.25 |
| Donbass | 130 | 12 | 10.83 |

Who is **relatively labour-abundant** as per H-O theory?
*Answer:* **Crimea** has the highest Labour/Land ratio (11.25), making it the most labour-abundant relative to land endowment.

**Problem 3 (Marsland vs Venusland):**

| | Marsland | Venusland |
|---|---|---|
| Wine (workers per bottle) | 15 | 10 |
| Cheese (workers per kg) | 10 | 4 |

- **Absolute advantage:** Venusland has absolute advantage in BOTH (fewer workers needed per unit)
- **Comparative advantage:** Marsland's opportunity cost of Wine = 15/10 = 1.5 Cheese; Venusland's = 10/4 = 2.5 Cheese. Marsland is relatively *less bad* at Wine → **Marsland exports Wine; Venusland exports Cheese**
- **Price ratio limits:** 10/15 < P_Wine/P_Cheese < 4/10 → **2/3 < P_W/P_C < 2.5**
