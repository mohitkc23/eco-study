## 1. The Foreign Exchange Market — Basics

### 1.1 What Is the Forex Market?

The **foreign exchange (forex/FX) market** is the global decentralised marketplace where currencies are bought and sold. It is the world's largest and most liquid financial market.

**Key facts:**
- Daily trading volume: approximately **$7.5 trillion** (as of 2022 BIS Triennial Survey)
- Operates **24 hours a day, 5 days a week** across global financial centres
- Major centres: London, New York, Tokyo, Singapore, Hong Kong
- **Over-the-counter (OTC)** market — not traded on a centralised exchange

### 1.2 The Exchange Rate — Definition

> The **exchange rate** is the price of one currency expressed in terms of another currency.

**Conventions:**

```
Direct quotation (from India's perspective):
e = ₹/$ = Number of rupees per 1 US dollar
e.g. e = 85 means 1 USD = ₹85

Indirect quotation (from India's perspective):
1/e = $/₹ = Number of dollars per 1 rupee
e.g. 1/85 = 0.0118 means 1 ₹ = $0.0118
```

**Depreciation vs Appreciation:**
- **Depreciation of INR:** e rises (e.g., ₹85 → ₹90 per USD) — rupee buys fewer dollars
- **Appreciation of INR:** e falls (e.g., ₹85 → ₹80 per USD) — rupee buys more dollars

### 1.3 Types of Exchange Rate Systems

```
EXCHANGE RATE REGIMES
│
├── FIXED (PEGGED) EXCHANGE RATE
│   Central bank commits to maintaining a specific exchange rate
│   Must intervene by buying/selling foreign currency
│   Example: Saudi Riyal pegged to USD at 3.75 SR/$
│
├── FLOATING (FLEXIBLE) EXCHANGE RATE
│   Exchange rate determined by market forces (demand and supply)
│   No official commitment to any specific level
│   Example: Indian Rupee, US Dollar, Euro
│
└── MANAGED FLOAT (DIRTY FLOAT)
    Market-determined BUT central bank intervenes periodically
    to smooth excessive volatility
    Example: India's actual practice (RBI intervenes)
    Also: China (CNY manages within a band)
```

## 2. Demand and Supply in the Forex Market

### 2.1 Demand for Foreign Currency (Demand for USD from India)

Demand for USD arises from:
- **Imports** — Indian importers need USD to pay foreign suppliers
- **Capital outflows** — Indian investors buying foreign assets
- **Tourism abroad** — Indians spending abroad need foreign currency
- **Debt repayment** — servicing USD-denominated external debt

### 2.2 Supply of Foreign Currency (Supply of USD into India)

Supply of USD arises from:
- **Exports** — Indian exporters receive USD from foreign buyers
- **Capital inflows** — FDI and FII investments flowing into India
- **Remittances** — NRIs sending money home
- **Tourism receipts** — Foreign tourists spending in India

### 2.3 Equilibrium Exchange Rate

```
            S_USD (Supply of USD into India)
Price of    │           /
USD in ₹    │          /
            │         / ← equilibrium e* = ₹85/$
         85─│────────X
            │       / \
            │      /   \
            │     /     \
            │    D_USD (Demand for USD by India)
            └─────────────────────────────── Quantity of USD
```

At **e* = ₹85/$**: Quantity of USD demanded = Quantity of USD supplied → **market clearing**

If USD demand rises (India imports more) → e rises (rupee depreciates) → makes imports more expensive → self-correcting mechanism

## 3. Uncovered Interest Parity (UIP)

### 3.1 The Core Idea

**Interest Rate Parity** states that in an integrated global capital market, the returns on comparable assets in different currencies should be equal (after accounting for expected exchange rate changes).

> **Uncovered Interest Parity (UIP):** The interest rate differential between two countries equals the expected rate of depreciation of the higher-interest-rate currency.

### 3.2 The UIP Condition

If you can invest:
- In India at interest rate **r_INR**
- In the USA at interest rate **r_USD**

For you to be indifferent between the two (no arbitrage profit), the following must hold:

```
(1 + r_INR) = (1 + r_USD) × (E_e / e)

Where:
  e    = current exchange rate (₹/$)
  E_e  = expected future exchange rate (₹/$)
  r_INR = Indian interest rate
  r_USD = US interest rate
```

**Simplified approximation:**

```
r_INR − r_USD ≈ (E_e − e) / e = Expected depreciation of INR
```

**Interpretation:**
> If India's interest rate is higher than the US rate by 5%, the market expects the rupee to **depreciate by approximately 5%** over the investment horizon.

### 3.3 Worked Example

- India interest rate: **7%**
- USA interest rate: **4%**
- Current exchange rate: **₹85/$**

UIP implies: Expected depreciation of rupee = 7% − 4% = **3%**

∴ Expected future exchange rate: 85 × (1 + 0.03) = **₹87.55/$**

**Policy implications of UIP:**
- A country cannot simultaneously have: (a) a higher interest rate than the world, (b) a stable exchange rate, and (c) open capital account — **the Impossible Trinity** (covered in Sessions 17-18)
- If India raises interest rates to fight inflation, UIP predicts the rupee will depreciate less than otherwise (because higher rates attract capital inflows)

### 3.4 The Carry Trade and UIP Violation

In practice, **UIP often does not hold** in the short run:
- The **carry trade** exploits interest rate differentials without offsetting depreciation
- Investors borrow in low-interest-rate currencies (USD, JPY) and invest in high-interest-rate currencies (INR, BRL)
- This works as long as the high-interest-rate currency does NOT depreciate as much as UIP predicts
- But carry trades unwind violently during crises (e.g., 2008, 2013 taper tantrum)

## 4. Purchasing Power Parity (PPP) — Long-Run Exchange Rate

### 4.1 The Law of One Price (Foundation)

> The **Law of One Price:** In competitive markets with no transport costs or trade barriers, identical goods should sell for the same price (when expressed in a common currency) in all countries.

**Example:** If a tonne of wheat costs ₹20,000 in India and $250 in the USA:
- Implied exchange rate = ₹20,000 / $250 = **₹80/$**
- If actual exchange rate is ₹85/$, wheat is cheaper in India (in dollar terms) → arbitrage → imports from India → prices converge

### 4.2 Absolute PPP

> **Absolute PPP:** The exchange rate between two currencies should equal the ratio of the two countries' **price levels**.

```
e = P_India / P_USA

Where:
  e = exchange rate (₹/$)
  P_India = Indian price level
  P_USA = US price level
```

### 4.3 Relative PPP (More Practical)

> **Relative PPP:** The **percentage change** in the exchange rate equals the **difference in inflation rates** between the two countries.

```
%Δe = π_India − π_USA

Where:
  %Δe    = percentage change in exchange rate (₹/$)
  π_India = inflation rate in India
  π_USA   = inflation rate in USA
```

**Worked Example:**
- India inflation: **5%/year**
- USA inflation: **2%/year**
- Current exchange rate: **₹85/$**

PPP-implied depreciation = 5% − 2% = **3%/year**

After 1 year: Expected exchange rate = 85 × (1.03) = **₹87.55/$**

**Key PPP insight:** Countries with **higher inflation** will see their currencies **depreciate** over time. This is consistent with UIP — higher inflation forces higher interest rates (Fisher effect), which in turn leads to depreciation.

### 4.4 The Big Mac Index — PPP in Practice

The Economist's **Big Mac Index** is a fun, practical application of PPP:
- A Big Mac costs $5.58 in the USA and ₹300 in India
- PPP-implied exchange rate = 300/5.58 = **₹53.76/$**
- Actual exchange rate = ₹85/$
- Implication: The Indian Rupee is **undervalued** relative to PPP by ~37%

**Why does PPP not hold in the short run?**
- Non-traded goods and services (haircuts, rent) — these do not face international arbitrage
- Transportation costs and tariffs
- Capital flows driving exchange rates independently of trade
- **Balassa-Samuelson Effect:** Productivity differences between traded and non-traded sectors create systematic deviations from PPP

### 4.5 Balassa-Samuelson Effect

> In rapidly growing emerging economies, **productivity in the traded goods sector** (manufacturing, exports) grows faster than in the **non-traded sector** (services, retail). This pushes up wages across the economy, making non-traded goods relatively more expensive — causing the real exchange rate to appreciate without violating PPP in the traded sector.

**Implication:** Emerging market currencies appear **undervalued** by PPP measures, but this partly reflects structural differences, not currency manipulation.

## 5. Real vs Nominal Exchange Rate

### 5.1 Nominal Exchange Rate

The exchange rate as quoted in financial markets — **₹85 per USD**.

### 5.2 Real Exchange Rate (RER)

> The **real exchange rate** adjusts the nominal rate for differences in price levels between countries. It measures the **competitiveness** of a country's goods.

```
RER = e × (P_foreign / P_domestic)
    = e × (P_USA / P_India)

Where e = nominal exchange rate (₹/$)
```

**Interpretation:**
- **RER rises** → domestic goods become relatively **cheaper** → exports more competitive
- **RER falls** → domestic goods become relatively **more expensive** → imports cheaper, exports less competitive

### 5.3 Real Effective Exchange Rate (REER)

A **trade-weighted average** of the real exchange rate against all trading partners. Used by RBI as a measure of India's overall external competitiveness.

## 6. Exchange Rate Overshooting (Dornbusch Model)

### 6.1 The Concept

In the short run, exchange rates **overshoot** their long-run equilibrium:
- A monetary expansion → short-run depreciation **beyond** the long-run PPP level
- Over time, the exchange rate gradually appreciates back to the new PPP equilibrium

**Why overshooting?**
- Goods prices adjust **slowly** (sticky prices)
- Financial asset prices (exchange rates) adjust **immediately**
- So the exchange rate bears the full initial adjustment burden, moving more than ultimately required

**Implication:** Exchange rate volatility can be high even when fundamentals are stable — a key challenge for monetary policy in open economies.

## 7. Factors That Determine the Exchange Rate — Summary

| Factor | Effect on Exchange Rate (e = ₹/$) | Direction |
|---|---|---|
| **India inflation ↑** (relative to USA) | Rupee depreciates | e ↑ |
| **India interest rates ↑** (capital inflows) | Rupee appreciates (short run) | e ↓ |
| **India exports ↑** (more USD supply) | Rupee appreciates | e ↓ |
| **India imports ↑** (more USD demand) | Rupee depreciates | e ↑ |
| **FDI/FII inflows ↑** | Rupee appreciates | e ↓ |
| **Capital flight / FII outflow** | Rupee depreciates | e ↑ |
| **India GDP growth ↑** (imports demand rises) | Rupee depreciates | e ↑ |
| **Risk aversion ↑ globally** | Safe-haven demand for USD → Rupee depreciates | e ↑ |
| **RBI sells USD (intervenes)** | Rupee appreciates | e ↓ |

## 8. Exchange Rate and Trade Policy

### 8.1 Currency Depreciation → Export Stimulus

A weaker rupee makes Indian exports cheaper in dollar terms → higher export demand:

```
Rupee depreciates: ₹85 → ₹90 per USD
Indian widget priced at ₹900:
  Before: costs US buyer $900/85 = $10.59
  After:  costs US buyer $900/90 = $10.00
→ Indian exports more competitive abroad
```

### 8.2 The J-Curve Effect

When a currency depreciates, the **trade balance initially worsens** before it improves:

```
TIME AFTER DEPRECIATION
│
│         Improvement
│        /
│       /
│      /
│─────/──── long run improvement
│    /
│   / ← initial worsening
│  /
│ /  J-Curve
└────────────────── Time
```

**Why the J-curve?**
- In the short run, import/export volumes are **fixed** (contracts, habits)
- Depreciation makes imports more expensive in rupees → import bill rises immediately
- Over time, volumes adjust — imports fall, exports rise → trade balance improves
- The lag is typically 6–18 months

### 8.3 Marshall-Lerner Condition

> Currency depreciation **improves** the trade balance **only if** the sum of the price elasticities of demand for exports and imports exceeds 1:

```
|η_X| + |η_M| > 1

Where:
  η_X = price elasticity of demand for exports
  η_M = price elasticity of demand for imports
```

If elasticities are low (inelastic demand), depreciation may worsen the trade balance permanently (the J-curve bottom never recovers).

## 9. India's Exchange Rate Policy in Practice

### 9.1 RBI's Approach

India officially has a **managed float** system:
- The rupee's value is primarily market-determined
- But the RBI intervenes in the forex market to:
  - Prevent excessive volatility
  - Build/replenish forex reserves
  - Prevent speculative attacks

### 9.2 India's Forex Reserves

India's forex reserves grew from:
- **$1.1 billion (June 1991)** — the crisis low
- To **~$620+ billion (2023–24)** — providing approximately 10–11 months of import cover

The dramatic build-up of reserves post-1991 reflects India's determination to never again face the vulnerability of 1991.

## 11. Exam-Ready Summary

### Key Theories of Exchange Rate Determination

| Theory | Timeframe | Key Variable | Core Prediction |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Law of One Price** | Instantaneous | Tradable goods prices | Price equalisation through arbitrage |
| **Absolute PPP** | Long-run | Price level ratio | e = P_domestic/P_foreign |
| **Relative PPP** | Long-run | Inflation differential | %Δe = π_d − π_f |
| **UIP** | Short-run | Interest rate differential | r_d − r_f = Expected depreciation |
| **Dornbusch Overshooting** | Short-run dynamics | Sticky prices + capital mobility | Short-run overshoot then reversion |

### Key Terms

- **Nominal exchange rate:** Quoted market rate (₹/$)
- **Real exchange rate:** Nominal rate adjusted for relative price levels
- **REER:** Trade-weighted average of real exchange rates
- **PPP:** Law that exchange rates should reflect relative purchasing power
- **UIP:** No-arbitrage condition linking interest rates and expected exchange rate changes
- **Carry trade:** Borrowing in low-rate currency, investing in high-rate currency
- **J-curve:** Trade balance initially worsens after depreciation before improving
- **Marshall-Lerner condition:** Depreciation improves trade balance only if |η_X| + |η_M| > 1
- **Managed float:** Market-determined exchange rate with periodic central bank intervention
