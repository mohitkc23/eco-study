## 1. External Accounts of a Country

Every nation maintains two types of external accounts. These two concepts are complementary — one is a **flow** concept and the other is a **stock** concept.

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                     EXTERNAL ACCOUNTS                           │
├──────────────────────────────┬──────────────────────────────────┤
│       BoP Account            │            IIP                   │
│  (Balance of Payments)       │  (International Investment       │
│                              │        Position)                 │
├──────────────────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────┤
│ Measures the international   │ Measures the total amount and    │
│ FLOW of goods, services,     │ distribution of assets abroad    │
│ and capital during a         │ and foreign assets in the        │
│ ONE-YEAR period.             │ nation at END of the year.       │
│                              │                                  │
│ → FLOW concept               │ → STOCK concept                  │
└──────────────────────────────┴──────────────────────────────────┘
```

### 1.1 Credit and Debit Transactions

| **Credit (+)** | **Debit (-)** |
|---|---|
| Receipt of payments **from** foreign sources | Payments **to** foreign sources |
| Exports of Goods and Services | Imports of Goods and Services |
| Unilateral transfers (gifts) **from** foreigners | Unilateral transfers (gifts) **to** foreigners |
| Financial **inflows**: Rise ↑ in foreign assets in a nation; Reduction ↓ in a nation's assets abroad | Financial **outflows**: Rise ↑ in a nation's assets abroad; Reduction ↓ in foreign assets in a nation |

**The key intuition:** Any transaction that brings money *into* the country is a Credit. Any transaction that sends money *out* is a Debit.

### 1.2 Double-Entry Book-Keeping

> In BoP accounting, each international transaction is recorded **twice** — once as a **credit** and once as a **debit**.

This is the same double-entry principle used in corporate accounting — every economic action has two sides.

## 2. The BoP Account: Full Structure

The Balance of Payments is composed of three major accounts:

```
Balance of Payments
│
├── CURRENT ACCOUNT
│   ├── Trade A/c (Goods)
│   │   ├── Exports
│   │   └── Imports
│   ├── Services
│   │   ├── Travel, Transport, Construction
│   │   ├── Insurance, Financial, IPRs, Telecom
│   │   └── NX Merchanting
│   ├── Primary Income
│   │   ├── Employee Compensation
│   │   └── Investment Income
│   └── Secondary Income
│       ├── Govt Transfers
│       └── Personal Transfers (Remittances)
│
├── CAPITAL ACCOUNT
│   ├── Non-produced, Non-financial asset disposal
│   └── Capital Transfers (debt forgiveness, migrants' transfers)
│
└── FINANCIAL ACCOUNT (formerly called Capital A/c)
    ├── Direct Investment
    │   ├── Equity Investment
    │   ├── Reinvested Earnings
    │   └── Debt Investment
    ├── Portfolio Investment
    │   ├── Equity Portfolio
    │   └── Debt Portfolio
    ├── Derivatives Investment
    ├── Other Financial Investment
    │   ├── ADR / GDR
    │   ├── External Loans (ECBs)
    │   ├── NRI Deposits
    │   └── Trade Finance
    └── RESERVE ASSETS (Changes)
        ├── Monetary Gold
        └── Foreign Currency Reserves
```

## 3. Current Account (with Accounting Examples)

> The current account in a country's Balance of Payments is a record of its transactions in goods, services, income, and current transfers with the rest of the world.

**Components:**
- **Goods** — exports and imports of tangible/physical items
- **Services** — trade in intangible items such as tourism, banking, IT, insurance, telecoms
- **Primary Income** — earnings from investments and employment abroad: interest, dividends, wages
- **Secondary Income (Current Transfers)** — unilateral transfers: remittances, foreign aid, gifts

### Accounting Example — Current Account

| Item | Debit (Dr) | Credit (Cr) |
|---|---|---|
| Goods Export | — | 1,000 |
| Services Import | 400 | — |
| Income Received | — | 150 |
| Current Transfers Paid | 50 | — |
| **Total** | **450** | **1,150** |

**Current Account Balance = 1,150 − 450 = +700 (Surplus)**

## 4. Capital Account (with Accounting Examples)

> The capital account records transactions that involve the **transfer of ownership of assets** between residents and non-residents, specifically non-produced, non-financial assets.

**Components:**
- **Capital transfers** — debt forgiveness, migrants' transfers, transfer of ownership of fixed assets
- **Acquisition/disposal of non-produced, non-financial assets** — patents, copyrights, trademarks

### Accounting Example — Capital Account

| Item | Debit (Dr) | Credit (Cr) |
|---|---|---|
| Debt Forgiveness Received | — | 200 |
| Migrants' Transfers Paid | 50 | — |
| Sale of Patent | — | 100 |
| Purchase of Trademark | 80 | — |
| **Total** | **130** | **300** |

---

## 5. Financial Account (with Accounting Examples)

> The financial account records transactions that **change ownership of financial assets and liabilities** between residents and non-residents.

**Components:**
- **Direct Investment** — purchase of a foreign company, setting up subsidiaries (>10% equity stake)
- **Portfolio Investment** — buying foreign stocks and bonds (<10% equity stake)
- **Other Investments** — loans, currency deposits, trade credits
- **Reserve Assets** — changes in official reserves held by the central bank

### Accounting Example — Financial Account

| Item | Debit (Dr) | Credit (Cr) |
|---|---|---|
| Direct Investment Abroad | 500 | — |
| Portfolio Investment Received | — | 300 |
| Loan Given to Foreign Entity | 200 | — |
| Increase in Reserve Assets | 100 | — |
| **Total** | **800** | **300** |

### 5.1 ADRs and GDRs — Important Instruments

#### GDRs (Global Depository Receipts)
- Equity instruments issued **abroad** by authorized overseas corporate bodies against shares/bonds of Indian companies held with domestic custodian banks
- Listed on **European** (Luxembourg, London) and **Asian** (Hong Kong, Singapore) exchanges
- Primarily sold to **foreign institutional investors** (FIIs)
- How GDRs work:
  1. Indian company issues corresponding shares to an overseas depository bank
  2. GDRs are freely transferable outside India; dividend paid in **Indian Rupees only**
  3. GDR trading happens on foreign stock exchange, between market makers on OTC basis
  4. GDR holder can instruct depository to convert to underlying shares and sell domestically

#### ADRs (American Depository Receipts)
- Negotiable instruments denominated in **USD**, issued by a **US Depository Bank**
- Non-US company deposits shares with the US depository bank and receives receipts → issues **American Depository Shares (ADS)**
- Key difference from GDRs: ADRs give access to **both retail and institutional investors** in the US; GDRs give access only to US institutional market
- Listed and traded on **NYSE and NASDAQ**

### 5.2 SDRs (Special Drawing Rights)

> **Special Drawing Rights (SDR)** is an international reserve asset, created by the **IMF in 1969** to supplement its member countries' official reserves.

Key facts:
- SDR is **neither a currency** nor a claim on the IMF — it is a potential claim on the freely usable currencies of IMF members
- Originally: **SDR = 0.89 grams of fine gold**
- Now: **1 SDR = Weighted basket of five currencies**

### SDR Currency Basket (2022 Weights)

| Currency | Weight (%) | Fixed Units of Currency |
|---|---|---|

| Euro | 29.31 | 0.37379 |

## 7. Open Macroeconomy — Essentials

### 7.1 The Circular Flow in a Macroeconomy

The macroeconomy involves three main actors — **Households, Government, and Firms** — interacting across three markets:

```
Household ←──────────────────────────────────→ Government ←────────→ Firms
    │                                               │                    │
    │ Taxes (T) →                                   │                    │
    │ ← Subsidy/Transfer                            │                    │
    │                                               │                    │
    │ Consumption Expenditure (C) →                 │ ← Gov. Expenditure(G)
    │                                               │ Public Savings (T-G)
    │                              Investment Expenditure (I) →          │
    │                                          Loans/Funds               │
    │                                                                     │
    │ ← Factor Payments (Wages, Rents, Profits) ←────────────────────────│
    │                                                                     │
National Income (Y) flows through: Goods Market | Financial Market | Labour Market
```

### 7.2 Open Economy National Income Identity

In an **open economy**, the fundamental expenditure-income identity is:

```
Y = C + I + G + X − M                          ... (1)
```

Rearranging:
```
Y − C − T       =    I    +   G − T    +   X − M
[Private Saving]    [Inv]  [Gov Deficit]  [Net Exports NX]
                                                            ... (2)
```

Further rearranged:
```
S_pvt − I  =  (G − T)  +  (X − M)
              [Gov.Def]   [Net Exports]                     ... (3)
```

### 7.3 Uses of Domestic Private Savings in an Open Economy

```
S_PVT  =  I  +  NX  +  Government Deficit
```

Private savings flow into:
1. **Investment Spending (I)** — financing capital formation by firms
2. **Net Exports (NX)** — financing exports
3. **Government Deficits** (negative public saving) — financing fiscal deficits

### 7.4 Importance of the Current Account Balance

The Current Account Deficit is structurally linked to both government and private sector deficits:

```
(M − X)           =   (G − T)        +   (I − S_pvt)
[Current A/c Deficit] [Govt. Deficit]    [Pvt Sector Deficit]
```

**Key insight:** A current account deficit is always the mirror image of domestic spending exceeding domestic income. It must be financed by capital inflows.

## 8. Summary: Key Formulas

| Formula | Meaning |
|---|---|
| `Y = C + I + G + X − M` | Open Economy GDP Identity |
| `S = I + CA` | Savings = Investment + Current Account |
| `S_pvt = I + NX + Gov. Deficit` | Uses of private savings |
| `(M − X) = (G − T) + (I − S_pvt)` | CA Deficit = Govt deficit + Private deficit |
| `CA = NX` | Current Account = Net Exports (simplified) |
