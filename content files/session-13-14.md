## 1. What Is a Payments Crisis?

A **payments crisis** (or **Balance of Payments crisis**) occurs when a country cannot meet its international financial obligations — it runs out of the foreign exchange needed to pay for imports, service external debt, or defend its exchange rate.

### 1.1 Sequence of a Typical BoP Crisis

```
1. TRIGGER
   ├── Sudden current account deterioration (import surge, export collapse)
   ├── Capital outflows (foreign investors pull money out)
   └── External shock (oil price spike, global recession)
         ↓
2. RESERVE DEPLETION
   Central bank uses forex reserves to defend the exchange rate
         ↓
3. CONFIDENCE CRISIS
   Markets see reserves falling → speculative attacks on currency
         ↓
4. CRISIS POINT
   Country cannot defend exchange rate OR service external debt
         ↓
5. ADJUSTMENT
   IMF bailout + structural adjustment programme
   Devaluation / depreciation of currency
   Austerity measures (import compression, fiscal tightening)
```

## 2. Case Study: India's BoP Crisis of 1991

*(Spiral Binder Reading)*

### 2.1 Background

By the late 1980s, India had accumulated a dangerous combination of:
- **Large fiscal deficits** (government borrowing to fund expenditure)
- **Rising current account deficits** (imports consistently exceeding exports)
- **High external debt**, much of it short-term
- **Low and dwindling foreign exchange reserves**

### 2.2 The Trigger

- **1990–91:** The Gulf War caused oil prices to spike sharply. India imported nearly 70% of its oil, making the current account deficit explode.
- Simultaneously, **Gulf remittances fell** as Indian workers were evacuated from Kuwait and Iraq
- Global confidence in India's creditworthiness collapsed
- **NRI (Non-Resident Indian) deposits** started flowing out

### 2.3 The Crisis Point

- By June 1991, India had **foreign exchange reserves of only ~$1.1 billion** — barely sufficient for 2 weeks of imports
- India was on the verge of **sovereign default** — unable to pay for imports
- India secretly **pledged its gold reserves** (400 tonnes) to the Bank of England and Bank of Japan as collateral to secure emergency loans

### 2.4 The Resolution

- **IMF Bailout:** India accepted a structural adjustment loan from the IMF (approximately $2.2 billion)
- **Devaluation:** The rupee was devalued by approximately 22% in two steps in July 1991
- **Economic Reforms:** Finance Minister Manmohan Singh's landmark budget (1991) began India's liberalisation — dismantling industrial licensing, reducing tariffs, opening to foreign investment
- **Long-term impact:** The 1991 crisis was the catalyst for India's economic transformation into a more open, market-oriented economy

### 2.5 Key Lessons

| Lesson | Implication |
|---|---|
| Unsustainable current account deficits eventually trigger a crisis | Monitor CAD/GDP ratio |
| Short-term external debt is dangerous | Prefer long-term FDI over volatile short-term flows |
| Adequate forex reserves are essential | RBI maintains 6–9 months import cover target |
| Structural reforms often emerge from crises | The 1991 crisis enabled what politics had blocked for decades |

## 3. Case Study: Indian Rupee Crisis of 2013

*(Ivey Publishing Case W15525 — Nidugala Ganesh K, Shukla Rashmi, Mostafa Romel)*

### 3.1 Context — The "Taper Tantrum"

In May 2013, US Federal Reserve Chairman **Ben Bernanke** hinted that the Fed might begin "tapering" (reducing) its Quantitative Easing (QE) programme — the large-scale bond-buying programme it had used to stimulate the US economy post-2008 financial crisis.

### 3.2 What is QE and Why Did It Matter for India?

**Quantitative Easing (QE)** = The Fed created money and bought US Treasury bonds and mortgage-backed securities → this pushed US interest rates to near-zero → investors sought higher returns in **emerging markets** like India.

**The "carry trade":** Borrow cheaply in USD at ~0% → invest in Indian bonds/equities yielding 7–9% → pocket the spread. This flooded India with "hot money" (short-term portfolio capital).

### 3.3 The Crisis Sequence

```
May 2013: Bernanke hints at QE tapering
         ↓
Investors anticipate: US interest rates will rise → USD will strengthen
→ "Hot money" flows out of emerging markets RAPIDLY
         ↓
India-specific vulnerabilities exposed:
• Wide current account deficit (CAD at ~4.8% of GDP — near crisis level)
• High inflation (CPI ~9-10%)
• Weak growth
• Large corporate external debt in USD
         ↓
Indian Rupee falls sharply:
From ₹54/$ → ₹68/$ within weeks (August 2013)
         ↓
RBI Response:
• Raised short-term interest rates aggressively
• Introduced FCNR(B) deposit scheme to attract NRI dollars
• Capital controls on outflows
         ↓
New RBI Governor Raghuram Rajan (September 2013):
• Credible policy signalling
• Focus on inflation targeting
• Gradual stabilisation of the rupee
```

### 3.4 Key Distinction: FDI vs Portfolio Investment

| | **Foreign Direct Investment (FDI)** | **Portfolio Investment (FPI/FII)** |
|---|---|---|
| **Definition** | Investment creating lasting interest (≥10% ownership) | Investment in securities (stocks, bonds) — no management control |
| **Stability** | **Stable** — hard to reverse, committed capital | **Volatile** — can exit rapidly ("hot money") |
| **Time horizon** | Long-term | Short-term |
| **India example** | Suzuki building Maruti plant | Foreign fund buying NSE-listed stocks |
| **Crisis role** | FDI rarely causes a crisis | Portfolio outflows can trigger currency crises |

### 3.5 Quality of Capital Flows

> **Excess global liquidity** — created by low interest rates in developed economies — sends large volumes of short-term portfolio capital into emerging markets. When this liquidity is withdrawn (as interest rates normalise), it can destabilise even fundamentally sound economies.

**The concept of "original sin" in capital flows:** Many emerging market countries can only borrow externally in foreign currency (USD). If their domestic currency depreciates, their debt burden (in domestic currency terms) explodes — creating a vicious cycle of depreciation → debt distress → more depreciation.

## 4. What Is a Reserve Currency?

> A **reserve currency** is a foreign currency held in significant quantities by central banks and financial institutions as part of their **foreign exchange reserves**. Used to facilitate international trade, settle international debts, and influence domestic exchange rates.

### 4.1 Why Countries Hold Reserves

Countries hold foreign exchange reserves to:
- **Weather economic shocks** — buffer against external crises
- **Pay for imports** — ensure steady supply even in a crisis
- **Service external debt** — meet repayment obligations
- **Moderate exchange rates** — intervene in currency markets
- **Signal creditworthiness** — assure creditors

> **The most important thing countries look for in reserves: the ability to access them in a moment of need — i.e., they must be able to accumulate or liquidate reserves quickly.**  
> — Prof. Kolukuluri (LN4)

## 5. The US Dollar as the World's Reserve Currency

### 5.1 Historical Origins — The Bretton Woods System (1944)

- **1944:** Forty-four Allied nations met at **Bretton Woods, New Hampshire** and agreed to the creation of the IMF and World Bank
- A new **exchange rate system** was established: each country pegged its currency to the **USD**, which was itself convertible to **gold at $35 per ounce**
- This replaced the gold standard (which collapsed during the Great Depression)
- Prevented "beggar-thy-neighbour" competitive devaluations of the 1930s

**The system's collapse:**
- By the 1960s, the USA had printed too many dollars — gold reserves were insufficient to back all outstanding dollars
- **August 1971:** President **Richard Nixon** suspended dollar-gold convertibility ("Nixon Shock")
- **1973:** The current system of mostly **floating exchange rates** took over

### 5.2 Why the Dollar Remains Dominant

Four pillars support the dollar's reserve currency status:

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│              WHY THE USD IS THE RESERVE CURRENCY                    │
├──────────────────────┬──────────────────────────────────────────────┤

│                      │ priced in USD. Every country needs USD to buy│
│                      │ oil. IMF study (Boz et al. 2022): overwhelming│
│                      │ share of trade invoiced in USD/Euro.         │
├──────────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  2. FOREX RESERVES   │ ~57.8% (2024) of global forex reserves are  │
│                      │ denominated in USD. These include USD currency│
│                      │ AND USD-denominated assets (US Treasuries).  │
```

### 5.4 Additional Dollar Properties (Bernanke, Brookings 2016)

Four key properties that make the USD attractive for international use:

1. **Stability of value** — since mid-1980s, Fed has maintained low and stable inflation
2. **Liquidity** — US Treasury market is the deepest and most liquid in the world; network externality (people trade Treasuries because others trade Treasuries)
3. **Safety** — US Treasuries are the global "safe haven" asset; USD appreciates during crises
4. **Lender of last resort** — During the 2008 financial crisis, the Fed instituted **currency swaps** with 14 central banks (including 4 in emerging markets), providing emergency dollar liquidity

## 6. Benefits and Costs of Dollar Dominance to the USA

### 6.1 The "Exorbitant Privilege"

> The term **"exorbitant privilege"** was coined by **Valéry Giscard d'Estaing**, then French Finance Minister, in the **1960s**, describing the advantages the USA enjoyed from issuing the world's reserve currency.

**Benefits to the USA:**

| Benefit | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Lower borrowing costs** | High global demand for USD and US Treasuries keeps US interest rates lower than they would otherwise be |
| **Cheap financing abroad** | When the USA ran trade surpluses historically, it could invest cheaply abroad — global hunger for USD means more purchasing power |
| **Reduced transaction costs** | US firms conduct international transactions in their own currency — no exchange rate risk |
| **Seigniorage** | USA effectively "exports" inflation by printing dollars held by other countries. Interest savings ~$20 billion/year |
| **Diplomatic weapon** | Dollar centrality enables powerful **financial sanctions** (see: Russia 2022) |

**On sanctions:**
> "Almost all trade done in US dollars, even trade among other countries, can be subject to US sanctions, because they are handled by so-called correspondent banks with accounts at the Federal Reserve."  
> — CFR Backgrounder (Siripurapu & Berman, 2023)

**Russia sanctions (2022):** Following Russia's invasion of Ukraine, unprecedented US sanctions cut Russia off from the dollar, **freezing $300 billion in Russian central bank assets** and triggering a default on sovereign debt.

### 6.2 Costs to the USA

> **"Strong Dollar ⟹ High exchange rate (e.g. 1 USD = ₹85) ⟹ Costly American Exports"**  
> — LN4INEDollarReserveCurrencyV2

| Cost | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **De-industrialisation** | Perpetual high demand for USD keeps the dollar stronger than it would otherwise be → US exports expensive → US manufacturing uncompetitive → **Rust Belt, Detroit** (car manufacturing) hollowed out |
| **Larger trade deficits** | Strong dollar makes imports cheap → US runs persistent trade deficits |
| **Constraint on monetary policy** | The Fed's decisions reverberate globally; international implications must be considered |

> **"The exorbitant privilege is not so exorbitant any more."**  
> — Ben Bernanke, Brookings, January 7, 2016

Bernanke argues that:
- US real interest rates are **no longer** significantly lower than other creditworthy nations
- Dollar's share of global reserves has **declined** (from ~71% in 2000 to ~58% in 2024)
- Competition from Euro and Yen has eroded the advantage

## 7. De-Dollarization

### 7.1 What Is De-Dollarization?

> **De-dollarization** refers to the **reduction of reliance** of foreign countries on the US dollar in international trade and finance.

> **Dollarization** (the opposite) is when a country recognises the US dollar as a medium of exchange alongside or in place of its domestic currency — typically when local currency has become unstable.

### 7.2 The BRICS Challenge

**BRICS** (originally Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa — now expanded to include Iran, Indonesia, Egypt, Ethiopia, Saudi Arabia, UAE — **10 countries total**):

- **Motive:** US-China trade war + US sanctions on China and Russia create incentives to transact outside the dollar system
- **BRICS Bank (New Development Bank — NDB):** Established 2015. Mobilises resources for infrastructure and sustainable development in BRICS and other emerging economies
- **Proposed BRICS currency:** Mooted but **nothing concrete materialised** as of 2026

### 7.3 Alternatives to the Dollar — And Their Problems

| Alternative | Current Share | Key Limitation |
|---|---|---|
| **Euro** | ~19.8% of reserves | No unified European bond market; fragmented sovereign debt market by country of issuance |
| **Chinese Renminbi** | ~2.18% of reserves | Capital controls; China controls money flows → **not freely usable** |
| **SDR (IMF)** | Small | Not an actual currency; can't be used in private transactions; US has de facto veto power in IMF |
| **Cryptocurrencies** | Negligible | Extreme volatility; no lender of last resort; constrains government crisis response |
| **BRICS currency** | None yet | Lack of robust central banks; structural challenges; divergent monetary policies among members |

### 7.4 The Empirical Verdict on De-Dollarization

> "Despite these stated aims and theoretical possibilities, a significant proportion of global currency invoicing in international trade is still denominated in US dollars — a pattern that has remained largely **unchanged post-COVID-19 pandemic** and the **Russian invasion of Ukraine**. The share of global forex reserves and forex turnover is still dominated by the US Dollar."  
> — Gerding and Hartley (2024), *Economics Letters*, Vol. 238

> **Close to 88 percent of over-the-counter (OTC) forex and derivatives instruments are in US dollars.**  
> — LN4INEDollarReserveCurrencyV2

### 7.5 The Future of the Dollar

Most experts agree:
- The dollar will **not be displaced** as the primary reserve currency **anytime soon**
- More likely: a **gradual multipolar currency system** where the dollar slowly shares influence
- This trend could be **accelerated** by aggressive US sanctions and growing US financial instability
- A weaker dollar role is **not necessarily catastrophic** for the USA, as long as it remains the primary global reserve

> CFR's Brad W. Setser: "It's more helpful to think of US Treasuries as the world's leading reserve asset. It's hard to compete with the dollar if you don't have a market analogous to the treasury market."

## 9. Exam-Ready Summary

### Key Definitions

- **Reserve currency:** Foreign currency held by central banks as part of official forex reserves; used to settle international transactions
- **Exorbitant privilege:** Benefits to the USA from issuing the world's primary reserve currency — lower borrowing costs, seigniorage, sanction power
- **Dollarization:** A country adopting USD as legal tender alongside domestic currency
- **De-dollarization:** Reducing reliance on USD in international trade and finance
- **Hot money:** Short-term portfolio capital flows that enter and exit emerging markets rapidly, driven by interest rate differentials; highly destabilising
- **Taper Tantrum (2013):** Market reaction to Fed's hinted reduction in QE, causing capital outflows from emerging markets
- **Original sin:** Emerging market countries can only borrow externally in foreign currency — creating a currency mismatch vulnerability
