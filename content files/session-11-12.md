## 2. Effects of an Import Tariff

### 2.1 The Pre-Tariff (Free Trade) Situation

Using a domestic market with:
- **Domestic Demand:** Downward sloping
- **Domestic Supply:** Upward sloping
- **World Price (P_world):** Lower than domestic autarky price

At free trade:
- **Consumer Surplus:** Triangle area AQB (large — consumers benefit from low world price)
- **Producer Surplus:** Area 0AC (small — domestic producers face world competition)
- **Imports:** CB (the gap between domestic demand and domestic supply at the world price)

```
P │A
  │ \  consumer surplus AQB
  │  Q─────────────B  ← free trade price
  │   ↑ prod.sur   ↑ imports = CB
  │  C              
  │   \
  └──────────────── Q
```

### 2.2 Post-Tariff Situation

When a tariff is imposed, the **domestic price rises** from the world price to: **P_int = world price + tariff**

Using the numerical example from the slides:
- Pre-tariff world price: implied low
- **Post-tariff price: P_int = 2** (world price was 1, tariff = 1)

**Key quantitative results:**

| Effect | Calculation | Result |
|---|---|---|
| **Consumption Effect** | Reduction in domestic demand: 80 − 50 = | **−30 units** |
| **Production Effect** | Rise in domestic supply: 20 − 10 = | **+10 units** |
| **Trade Effect** | Fall in imports: (80−10) − (50−20) = 70 − 30 = | **−40 units... [60−30 = 30 unit fall]** |
| **Government Revenue** | New imports × tariff = 30 × 1 = | **Revenue = 30** |
| **Deadweight Loss** | Area CJM + Area NBH = ½×10×1 + ½×20×1 = | **= 15** |

*Using the slide's coordinate labels: C, M, J, N, B, H are points on the supply-demand diagram*

### 2.3 The Four Effects of a Tariff — A Summary

> **Resulting Effects of Tariff:**
> 1. **Consumption effect:** Reduction in domestic consumption (consumers buy less at higher tariff price)
> 2. **Production effect:** Expansion of domestic production (domestic producers benefit from higher protected price)
> 3. **Trade effect:** Decline in imports (gap between domestic demand and supply narrows)
> 4. **Revenue effect:** Revenue collected by the government (= new import volume × tariff rate)

### 2.4 Welfare Analysis

```
Area of Consumer Surplus LOST = ABHG (large trapezoid)
  This breaks down into:
  ├── Area ACJG → Transferred to PRODUCERS (Subsidy Effect)
  ├── Area CMJ  → Deadweight Loss (production inefficiency)
  ├── Area JMNH → Transferred to GOVERNMENT (Revenue Effect)
  └── Area NBH  → Deadweight Loss (consumption inefficiency)

Net National Loss = CMJ + NBH = DEADWEIGHT LOSS
```

**Deadweight loss calculation:**
```
DWL = ½ × (production distortion) × tariff + ½ × (consumption distortion) × tariff
    = ½ × 10 × 1 + ½ × 20 × 1
    = 5 + 10 = 15
```

**Full Surplus Table:**

| Actor | Pre-Tariff | Post-Tariff | Net Effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| Consumer Surplus | x + f + a + b + c + d | x + f | **−(a+b+c+d)** |
| Producer Surplus | e | a + e | **+a** |
| Government Revenue | 0 | c | **+c** |
| **Net National W/L** | x+f+a+b+c+d+e | x+f+a+e+c | **−(b+d) = DWL** |

> **The areas (b+d) represent pure deadweight loss — they go to NO ONE. This is the economic cost of the tariff to the importing nation.**

## 3. Effective Rate of Protection (ERP)

### 3.1 The Concept

The **nominal tariff rate** is the stated tariff on the final imported good. However, this overstates or understates the actual protection given to the domestic **value-added** in production.

> When a nation imposes a **lower tariff on imported inputs** than on the final commodity produced with those inputs, the **rate of effective protection exceeds the nominal tariff rate**.

**Why this matters:** A final-goods tariff of 10% can provide 50% protection to the value-added in production if the imported inputs used are taxed at only 2%. Developing countries often structure their tariff schedules this way.

### 3.2 The ERP Formula

> **Rate of Effective Protection:**

```
g = (t − Σ(a_i × t_i)) / (1 − Σa_i)
```

Where:
- **g** = rate of effective protection (what we want to find)
- **t** = nominal tariff rate on the **final commodity**
- **a_i** = ratio of cost of imported input *i* to the price of final commodity (with no tariff)
- **t_i** = nominal tariff rate on imported input *i*

### 3.3 Worked Example

Suppose:
- A car is priced at $10,000 in the world market
- Steel (imported input) costs $3,000 (a = 0.3)
- Nominal tariff on car = 20% (t = 0.20)
- Nominal tariff on steel = 5% (t_i = 0.05)

```
g = (0.20 − 0.3 × 0.05) / (1 − 0.3)
  = (0.20 − 0.015) / 0.70
  = 0.185 / 0.70
  = 0.264 = 26.4%
```

The **effective protection rate is 26.4%** — much higher than the nominal 20% tariff on cars.

**Insight:** Effective protection can be very high even with modest nominal tariffs, especially when imported inputs carry low tariffs. This is a critical distinction for policy analysis.

## 4. Import Quotas

### 4.1 Definition and Mechanics

> **An import quota** is a direct **quantitative restriction** on the amount of a commodity allowed to be imported or exported. Import quotas limit imports to specified levels with **certainty**, while the trade effect of an import tariff may be uncertain.

**Key features:**
- Enforced via **import licensing requirements**
- Intended to protect a declining Balance of Payments (BoP)
- Creates **quota rents** — the difference between the domestic price and world price, times the quota volume

### 4.2 Why Quotas Are Worse than Tariffs (in Economic Terms)

| Issue | Tariff | Import Quota |
|---|---|---|
| Revenue for government | ✓ Government gets tariff revenue | ✗ Revenue goes to license holders (quota rents) |
| Market mechanism | Preserved (price system works) | **Replaced** — quantity is fixed, not price |
| Transparency | High | Low |
| Efficiency | Less distortionary | **More distortionary** |
| Corruption risk | Lower | **Higher** — license allocation invites rent-seeking |

> **"Import quotas replace the market mechanism, resulting in waste and possible corruption."**  
> — Prof. Kolukuluri

**Rent-seeking:** Monopoly profits lead firms to **lobby for licenses** rather than invest in productive activity. This is pure rent-seeking — a social waste.

## 5. Voluntary Export Restraints (VERs)

### 5.1 Definition

> **Voluntary Export Restraints (VERs):** An importing country **induces** another nation to **reduce its exports voluntarily**, under threat of higher trade restrictions. Sometimes called **orderly marketing arrangements (OMAs)**.

### 5.2 Key Characteristics

- The word "voluntary" is a misnomer — the exporting country agrees under **threat** of worse restrictions
- Allow industrial nations to **appear to support free trade** while actually restricting it
- **Less effective in limiting imports than import quotas** because exporters tend to fill the quota with **higher quality, higher priced goods** over time (known as "quality upgrading")

### 5.3 Case Study: Plaza Accords and Japanese Stagnation

**Background:** By the early 1980s, Japanese car manufacturers (Toyota, Honda, Nissan) had gained massive US market share. US automakers (Detroit — Ford, GM, Chrysler) were struggling.

**The VER:** In 1981, Japan agreed to limit car exports to the USA to approximately 1.68 million vehicles per year.

**Outcome:**
- Japan shifted from exporting small cheap cars to exporting **higher-value, higher-margin vehicles** (Accord, Camry, Lexus category began here)
- Japan also shifted production **to the USA** (Honda's Marysville Ohio plant)
- The **Plaza Accord (1985):** US, Japan, West Germany, UK, France agreed to intervene in currency markets to **depreciate the USD** against the Yen, making Japanese exports more expensive
- Post-Plaza Accord, the Yen appreciated sharply (from ~240¥/$ to ~120¥/$), causing Japan's export-led growth model to falter
- This contributed to Japan's asset bubble (1986-1991) and subsequent "Lost Decade"

## 6. Dumping

### 6.1 Definition

> **Dumping:** The export of a commodity at **below cost**, or the sale of a commodity at a **lower price abroad than domestically**.

### 6.2 Three Types of Dumping

| Type | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Persistent Dumping** | Continuous tendency of a domestic monopolist to maximise total profits by selling at a **higher price domestically and lower price abroad** | A steel company sells at ₹60,000/tonne domestically and $600/tonne (equivalent ₹51,000) internationally |
| **Predatory Dumping** | **Temporary** sale at below cost or lower price abroad to **drive foreign producers out of business** | Once rivals are eliminated, raise prices to monopoly level |
| **Sporadic Dumping** | **Occasional** sale at below cost abroad to **unload a surplus** without reducing domestic prices | Seasonal agricultural surplus; sell abroad at discount |

**Why predatory dumping is particularly harmful:** It can destroy entire domestic industries in the importing country, creating long-term dependence on the foreign monopolist.

**Anti-dumping measures:** Most WTO member countries can impose **anti-dumping duties** if they can prove dumping is occurring and causing material injury to domestic industry.

## 7. Export Subsidies

### 7.1 Definition

> **Export Subsidies:** The granting of **tax relief to exporters** or **subsidised loans to foreign buyers** to stimulate a nation's exports.

**Characteristics:**
- Can be regarded as a **form of dumping** (selling below true cost when the subsidy is factored in)
- Export subsidies are **illegal under international agreement** (WTO Agreement on Subsidies and Countervailing Measures — ASCM)
- But are **often used in disguised form** — tax rebates, soft loans, favourable infrastructure pricing, state-owned enterprise cross-subsidies

**WTO-permitted exceptions:** Domestic support subsidies in agriculture remain a major grey area, particularly for developed countries.

## 8. The Infant Industry Argument

### 8.1 The Core Argument

> **Infant Industry Argument:** Temporary trade protection is justified to **establish and protect a domestic industry** during its "infancy" until it can:
> - Meet foreign competition on equal terms
> - Achieve **economies of scale**
> - Develop the comparative advantage that factor endowments suggest it should have

**Attributed to:** Alexander Hamilton (US Treasury Secretary, 1791) and Friedrich List (German economist, 19th century). The same argument was used by Japan (steel, cars, electronics) and South Korea (POSCO, Hyundai, Samsung) post-WW2.

### 8.2 The Classic Counterpoint

> **"What trade protection can do, an equivalent production subsidy to the infant industry can do better."**  
> — Standard economic critique

**Why a production subsidy is better than a tariff for infant industry support:**

| | **Tariff** | **Production Subsidy** |
|---|---|---|
| Benefit to domestic producers | ✓ | ✓ |
| Cost to domestic consumers | Yes — higher prices | **No** — consumers still pay world price |
| Revenue for government | ✓ | Government must **pay** the subsidy |
| Distortion | Consumer + production | **Production only** |
| Net efficiency loss | Higher | **Lower** |

**Additional concerns with infant industry protection:**
1. **How long is "temporary"?** Protected industries rarely volunteer to give up protection
2. **Picking winners:** Governments often pick the wrong industries to protect
3. **Vested interests:** Protected industries lobby to maintain protection indefinitely
4. **Retaliation:** Trading partners may retaliate with their own protection

## 9. Summary: Trade Policy Tools Compared

| Policy Tool | Effect on Domestic Price | Revenue to Govt? | DWL? | Key Concern |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Free Trade** | World price | None | None | Adjustment costs |
| **Tariff** | World + tariff | ✓ Yes | Yes (b+d) | DWL |
| **Import Quota** | Higher (quota rent) | No (to licensees) | Yes + rents | Corruption |
| **VER** | Higher (similar to quota) | No (to exporters) | Yes | Quality upgrading |
| **Export Subsidy** | Lower for foreign buyer | No (costs govt) | Yes | WTO illegality |
| **Anti-dumping duty** | Raises import price | ✓ Yes | Possible | Misuse as protectionism |

## 10. Exam-Ready Summary

### Key Formulas and Numbers to Remember

```
Effective Rate of Protection:
g = (t − Σa_i t_i) / (1 − Σa_i)

Deadweight Loss from Tariff:
DWL = Production Loss + Consumption Loss
    = ½ × (production effect) × tariff + ½ × (consumption effect) × tariff

Tariff Revenue = (post-tariff imports) × tariff rate
```

### Key Definitions

- **Nominal tariff:** Stated rate of tax on the imported final good
- **Effective protection rate (g):** Actual protection given to domestic value-added — always higher than nominal rate when inputs have lower tariffs than final goods
- **Deadweight loss:** Net efficiency loss from tariff — areas (b+d) — go to NO one
- **Import quota:** Quantitative limit on imports; creates quota rents
- **VER:** "Voluntary" export restraint negotiated under threat; leads to quality upgrading
- **Predatory dumping:** Temporary below-cost export to destroy foreign rivals
- **Infant industry argument:** Temporary protection to allow an industry to mature
