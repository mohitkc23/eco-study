## 2. The Spice Route

### 2.1 Arab Traders as the Conduit

- **Arab traders** were the primary conduit for Indian trade with Europe from **600 BC**
- Arabs constructed elaborate **myths and stories** about the origin of spices to protect their monopoly

### 2.2 The Cinnamologus Myth
- **Origin:** Arab traders, ~2,500 years ago, invented the myth of the *cinnamologus* — a fictional bird that nested with delicate cinnamon sticks
- **Clever strategy:** The fable suggested obtaining cinnamon by baiting the cinnamologous with meat, causing its cinnamon nests to collapse
- **Purpose:** The fictional creature never existed — it was a carefully crafted fable to enhance the mystery and value of the cinnamon trade, and prevent Europeans from going to the source
- **Source:** BBC — *The Flavours that Shaped the World*

### 2.3 The Margins Were Enormous

> **1 kg of pepper:** from **2 grams of Silver** at source in India → **30 grams of Silver** in Europe.

This markup of **15x** illustrates why the spice trade was the most profitable commerce of the ancient world.

## 3. The Ancient Silk Road

- Interconnected China to Central Asia, Persia, Arabia, and eventually Europe
- Multiple branches: land routes through the Tarim Basin and Ferghana Valley; sea routes through Southeast Asia
- Traded: silk, ceramics, spices, precious metals, glass, horses
- Also facilitated exchange of religions, ideas, and technologies

*Source: UNESCO Silk Roads Programme*

## 5. (Gradual) Demise of Free Trade

### 5.1 The Closure of the Land Route — 1453

Three converging forces ended the era of relatively open ancient trade:

1. **Ottoman Conquest of Constantinople (1453):** The Ottoman Turks captured Constantinople (Istanbul) from the Holy Roman Empire (Byzantine), closing the land route to Asia for European merchants
2. **Reconquista in Spain:** Catholic Spain expelled Jews and Muslims after their re-conquest of Granada, disrupting established mercantile networks
3. **Social and Political changes in European class structure:** Power shifted from landed feudal barons to urban merchants; this created new political pressure to control trade flows

*Visual: The Bosphorus — the land link between Asia and Europe — was now controlled by the Ottomans*

---

## 6. The Age of Discovery

### 6.1 Columbus and the Western Voyages

> **1492:** Christopher Columbus (an Italian) approached Queen Isabella of Spain for ships and escort to find a **sea route to the East** (India), bypassing the Ottoman-controlled land routes.

Columbus sailed West, intending to reach India. He reached the Americas instead.

- **1498:** Portuguese explorer **Vasco Da Gama** successfully found the sea route to India, arriving at Calicut (Kozhikode). This broke the Arab and Ottoman stranglehold on the Spice Route.

> **The search for a direct route — i.e. no middlemen — to find the source of spices stimulated European voyages that turned into colonial conquests.**  
> — *The Spice Trade & the Age of Exploration*

### 6.2 The Discovery of the Americas — Key Sequence

1. **Columbus lands in the Americas (1492)** — changes global trade routes and dynamics permanently
2. **Gold and Silver abundance in New Spain:** Vast reserves of gold and silver were revealed in Central and Southern America, notably in New Spain (present-day Mexico) and the Incan Empire
3. **Incan gold shipped to Spain:** Conquistadores including **Hernán Cortés** conquered the Incan Empire and transported enormous quantities of precious metals to Spain
4. **Gold and Silver push inflation:** The influx of precious metals into Europe caused spending and inflation — this contributed to early understanding of the **Quantity Theory of Money**
5. **Asset holding becomes valuable:** Gold and silver were increasingly seen as stores of wealth to hold for future sale
6. **Gold as "Sinews of War":** Precious metals funded armies, enabling states to maintain military forces and pursue war
7. **Merchants and the State value gold:** Both merchants and the state recognised the strategic importance of accumulating gold and silver as symbols of power and economic stability

- **Company employees became the new aristocrats** — enormous personal wealth was generated

## 8. Origins and Ideology of Mercantilism

### 8.1 Historical Context (15th–18th Century Europe)

```
Feudal Barons → Princely States → Nation States
              Power shifted from landed aristocracy → Merchants
```

Merchants formed **Guilds**, generated wealth, and shared it with the State. This political alliance between merchant capital and state power gave birth to mercantilist ideology.

### 8.2 What is Mercantilism?

> **Merchant Capitalism = Mercantilism**

**Core philosophy: "Beggar thy trade partner"**

| Mercantilist Principle | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Export > Import | Run a permanent trade surplus |
| Economic Nationalism | Prioritise national economic interests above all |
| Economic Protectionism | Shield domestic industries from foreign competition |
| Accumulate Gold & Silver | Bullion = national wealth and power |
| Trade is Zero-Sum | One nation's gain is another's loss |

### 8.3 The Mercantilist Quote

> **"Gold is a wonderful thing! Whoever possesses it is a master of everything he desires. With gold, one can even get souls into paradise!"**  
> — Christopher Columbus

### 8.4 Key Mercantilist Thinker: Thomas Mun (1571–1641)

- **Director of the East India Company**
- Key prescriptions:
  - Bring **bullion (gold/silver)** in exchange for exports
  - Refrain from excessive domestic consumption of foreign goods
  - Try to meet neighbours' needs (Export as much as possible)
  - Compete to **eliminate rival exporters**

### 8.5 Challenge to Mercantilism

Two major intellectual challenges emerged:

1. **Physiocrats in France:** Argued for *laissez-faire* — free markets, especially in agriculture. Believed agricultural surplus, not trade balances, was the source of national wealth.

2. **Adam Smith in Britain:** Published *An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations* (1776)

> **"It is the maxim of every prudent master of a family, never to attempt to make at home what it will cost him more to make than to buy. The tailor does not attempt to make his own shoes, but buys them of the shoemaker. The shoemaker does not attempt to make his own clothes but employs a tailor."**  
>  
> **"What is prudence in the conduct of every private family, can scarce be folly in that of a great kingdom."**  
> — Adam Smith, *An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations*, 1776

## 9. Coercive Trade

### 9.1 The Core Logic of Coercive Trade

Mercantilism held that **trade is a zero-sum game**. Once this ideology took hold, the logical extension was coercion — if trade enriches you at my expense, I will use force to ensure the terms favour me.

```
Mercantilist Ideology  →  Trade is Zero-Sum  →  Trade to Coercion
```

### 9.2 Case Study: Plunder of Bengal (India)

- **Battle of Plassey (1757):** East India Company's military victory
- **Robert Clive becomes Governor of Bengal — 1757**
- **Permanent Settlement — 1765:** EIC gains economic power; systematic extraction of Bengal's wealth begins
- British enact laws that **prohibit import of Indian cloth** (to protect British textile mills), but simultaneously promote British exports to India
- Classic mercantilist policy: suppress the colony's exports, flood it with your own manufactured goods

### 9.3 Case Study: The Opium Wars — China (1839–42 and 1856–60)

**The trade imbalance problem:**
- Britain bought enormous quantities of Chinese tea, silk, and porcelain
- China, in turn, wanted little from Britain — it was largely self-sufficient
- Britain was haemorrhaging silver to China, creating a severe trade deficit
- The only commodity that China would buy was **opium**, grown in British-controlled India

**The conflict:**
- The **Qing dynasty** officially barred opium imports (recognising the social devastation it caused)
- But the British needed opium trade to balance their payments with China
- China was **politically weakened** by internal divisions
- Britain's ambassador was required to **kowtow** (prostrate) in the Qing court — symbolising China's superiority — which humiliated Britain diplomatically

**The war:**
- Britain goes to war (Opium Wars) and wins trade concessions
- The wars were **deeply unpopular** in Britain — William Gladstone, the future Prime Minister, called them "a war more unjust in its origin than the Opium War has not been waged"
- Britain gains control of **Hong Kong** on the Pearl River Delta
- China forced to open treaty ports and grant extraterritorial rights to foreigners

*Source: HBS Case — The First Opium War and Global Free Trade (Jeremy Friedman & Allison Lazarus, 9-722-052)*

### 9.4 Case Study: Forced Opening of Japan (1852–1854)

- **1852:** Commodore **Matthew C. Perry** of the US Navy was assigned a mission by President **Millard Fillmore** to force the opening of Japanese ports to American trade through **gunboat diplomacy**
- Japan (under the Edo Shogunate) had been in **sakoku** (closed country) policy for over 200 years
- The Japanese relented under threat of US Navy's black ships (steam-powered warships)
- **Unequal treaties** were signed between Japan and foreign powers, opening Japanese ports
- This humiliation led directly to the **Meiji Restoration** — Japan's dramatic modernisation programme to avoid the fate of colonised China and India

## 10. The Pattern of Mercantilism — A Critical Summary

> **"Mercantilism had an aggressive and war-like element in it."**  
> — John Kenneth Galbraith, *A History of Economics: The Past as the Present*

Key observations on the mercantile system:

| Claim | Reality |
|---|---|
| European merchants claimed "Free Trade" | In practice, it was the **opposite** — they prevented free trade |
| Trade for mutual benefit | Trade was **one-sided**, focused on amassing wealth |
| Competition welcomed | Merchants had a **negative view of competition**, both domestic and foreign |
| Wage impact | **No positive impact on wages** — politically feasible because democracy didn't exist in the modern sense |
| Trade method | **Brute force** was used to restrict imports from rivals; force was also used to promote own exports |

## 11. Modern Day Mercantilism — China

An important contemporary application of mercantilist thinking:

- Chinese acceptance of free trade is **not to improve consumer surplus (welfare)** of Chinese citizens
- It is primarily to improve **Producer's surplus** (serving the interests of the Chinese Communist Party and state-linked enterprises)
- China distinguishes between:
  - **Chinese firms:** Chinese-owned enterprises
  - **Establishments:** Foreign-owned companies operating from China
- This selective participation in global trade while maintaining domestic market controls reflects **neo-mercantilist** thinking — sometimes called **"innovation mercantilism"** (to be covered later in the course)

## 12. Savings & Investment in an Open Economy (Closing Framework)

This slide bridges history to modern macroeconomics — why do nations trade?

### Basic Identity

```
Y = C + I + G + NX

S = Y − C − G
⟹ S = I + NX
```

**Interpretations:**
1. **Savings is used for investment (I) and net exports (NX)**
2. The **current account balance (CA) = NX**, which gives: **S = I + CA**

### Extended Identity (Private + Public Savings)

```
S_pvt = I − S_gov + NX

Components:
  Private Savings (S_pvt)   → Financing investment
  Government Deficit (−S_gov) → Government may absorb private saving if running deficit
  Net Exports (NX/CA)       → If CA > 0: foreigners borrow from domestic residents or sell assets
```
